Granger Causality: Predictive Relationships in Time Series

Posted on June 13, 2025 | Category: Econometrics, Time Series

In economics and many other fields, we often want to know not just whether two variables move together, but whether changes in one actually help predict changes in the other. This is where Granger causality comes in—a powerful tool for uncovering predictive relationships in time series data.

What Is Granger Causality?

Introduced by Nobel laureate Clive Granger in 1969, Granger causality is a statistical hypothesis test that asks: Does knowing the past values of time series X improve our forecast of time series Y, beyond what we could predict using only Y’s own past? If so, we say that “X Granger-causes Y”1.

Key Principle:
X Granger-causes Y if predictions of Y based on its own past values and on the past values of X are better than predictions of Y based only on Y’s own past values.

It’s important to note that Granger causality does not mean true causality in the philosophical sense. Rather, it’s about predictive causality—whether one variable contains unique information about the future of another2.

How Does the Granger Causality Test Work?

  1. Build two forecasting models for Y:
    • Model 1: Predict Y using only its own past values (an autoregression).
    • Model 2: Predict Y using its own past values and the past values of X.
  2. Compare forecasting accuracy:
    • If Model 2 yields significantly better forecasts (lower prediction error), we say X Granger-causes Y.
Typical Model:
$$ Y_t = a_0 + a_1 Y_{t-1} + \dots + a_p Y_{t-p} + b_1 X_{t-1} + \dots + b_q X_{t-q} + \varepsilon_t $$

If the coefficients $b_1, \dots, b_q$ are jointly statistically significant, X is said to Granger-cause Y.

Example: Consumer Confidence and Retail Sales

Suppose you want to know if changes in consumer confidence (X) can help predict retail sales (Y). If adding past values of consumer confidence to your model for retail sales significantly improves forecast accuracy, then consumer confidence Granger-causes retail sales3.

Important Caveats and Limitations

  • Stationarity: The time series must be stationary (their statistical properties do not change over time) for the test to be valid4.
  • No true causality: Granger causality means “X precedes and predicts Y,” not that X truly causes Y. There could be hidden variables or feedback loops5.
  • Linear and lagged relationships only: The standard test detects only linear and lagged predictive relationships, not instantaneous or nonlinear effects.

Why Is Granger Causality Useful?

Despite its limitations, Granger causality is widely used in economics, finance, neuroscience, and many other fields because it is simple, interpretable, and computationally efficient. It helps researchers move beyond mere correlation and rigorously test for predictive links in time series data6.
