The Post-COVID Inflation Persistence: Structural Hysteresis or Transitory Shock?

Frédéric Mirindi | October 15, 2025 | Macroeconomics

T

he global economy is currently navigating a precarious equilibrium. The swift recovery following the pandemic-induced contraction unleashed an inflationary wave whose duration and intensity confounded central bank forecasts. The prevailing narrative—that of "transitory" supply-chain bottlenecks—has increasingly given way to fears of entrenched, secular stagnation accompanied by stubborn price instability.

This analysis employs a Vector Error Correction Model (VECM) applied to harmonized G7 CPI and PPI data to decompose the drivers of this persistence. The central inquiry is whether we are witnessing a temporary deviation from the low-inflation anchor of the last decade, or a structural regime shift driven by de-globalization and labor market hysteresis.

The Decomposition of Shocks

Our model isolates two distinct vectors: exogenous supply-side constraints (energy shocks, semiconductor shortages) and endogenous demand-pull factors (fiscal stimulus, excess savings). While 2021-2022 data clearly points to supply dominance, the 2024-2025 residuals indicate a worrying trend: the "stickiness" of core inflation is now primarily driven by service-sector wages and unanchored consumer expectations.

"The Phillips curve, long thought dormant, appears to be re-asserting itself with non-linear convexity in the post-pandemic labor market."

Policy Implications

If inflation is indeed structural, the "soft landing" scenario engineered by the Federal Reserve and the Bank of Canada faces significant headwinds. The data suggests that bringing inflation back to the 2% target may require a higher sacrifice ratio—meaning higher unemployment—than current policy models admit. We must prepare for a period of higher-for-longer interest rates, not as a temporary cyclical tool, but as a necessary corrective to re-anchor long-term price stability.

Protectionism and the Canadian Pivot: Analyzing Trade Diversion Effects

Frédéric Mirindi | November 02, 2025 | Trade Policy

C

anada's economic destiny has historically been tethered to the whims of its southern neighbor. The recent turn toward protectionism in Washington represents a profound exogenous shock to this relationship. Tariffs are not merely taxes on imports; they are signals that distort capital allocation and disrupt deeply integrated cross-border supply chains.

This briefing examines the "trade diversion" effects currently rippling through the Canadian manufacturing and energy sectors. Using a Gravity Model of Trade augmented with tariff variables, we estimate the elasticity of Canadian exports to recent US duties.

Exchange Rate Pass-Through

Conventional wisdom holds that a depreciating Canadian dollar (CAD) buffers exporters against foreign tariffs. However, our GARCH analysis of CAD/USD volatility reveals a breakdown in this relationship. The uncertainty generated by arbitrary tariff schedules has dampened investment more than the weaker currency has stimulated sales. This phenomenon—"elasticity pessimism"—suggests that Canadian exporters are price-takers in a market where political risk now carries a higher premium than price competitiveness.

The Sectoral Decoupling

The automotive sector faces an existential pivot. With "Buy American" provisions excluding Canadian inputs, the integrated Great Lakes manufacturing ecosystem is fracturing. Conversely, this pressure acts as a catalyst for Canada to aggressively pursue CETA (Europe) and CPTPP (Asia-Pacific) markets. The data is clear: diversification is no longer a luxury strategy, but a prerequisite for sovereign economic stability.
