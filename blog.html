Understanding Auction Mechanisms: First-Price vs. Second-Price Sealed Bids

Posted on April 8, 2025 | Category: Microeconomics, Game Theory

Auctions are common mechanisms for allocating goods. In sealed-bid auctions, participants submit bids simultaneously. Let's analyze bidder strategy in two key types, assuming independent private values where each bidder $i$ knows their own value $v_i$ but only the distribution of others' values.

1. First-Price Sealed-Bid Auction

The highest bidder wins and pays their own bid amount, $b_i$.

Strategic Considerations

Bidding your true value ($b_i = v_i$) is generally not optimal. If you win, your payoff is $v_i - b_i = v_i - v_i = 0$. Bidding lower ($b_i < v_i$) creates a potential positive payoff but reduces the probability of winning.

Equilibrium Bidding (Example)

Assume $n$ bidders with values $v_i$ independently drawn from $U[0, 1]$. In a symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium, a bidder with value $v_i$ submits the optimal bid $b_i^*(v_i)$:

$$ b_i^*(v_i) = \frac{n-1}{n} v_i $$

This involves "shading" the bid below the true value. Deriving this involves maximizing expected payoff $E[\text{Payoff}] = (v_i - b_i) \times P(\text{Win} | b_i)$.

2. Second-Price Sealed-Bid Auction (Vickrey Auction)

The highest bidder wins but pays the amount of the *second-highest* bid.

Dominant Strategy: Truthful Bidding

Bidding your true value ($b_i = v_i$) is a weakly dominant strategy. This means $b_i = v_i$ yields an expected payoff at least as high as any other bid $b_i'$, regardless of other players' bids.

Proof Outline (Why $b_i = v_i$ is optimal)

Let $v_i$ be your value and $B_{-i} = \max_{j \neq i} \{b_j\}$ be the highest bid among others. Consider bidding $b_i$.

  1. Case 1: Consider bidding $b_i' > v_i$ (Overbidding).
    • If $B_{-i} \ge b_i'$, you lose. Payoff = 0. (Same as bidding $v_i$).
    • If $v_i \le B_{-i} < b_i'$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff is $v_i - B_{-i} \le 0$. Bidding $v_i$ means losing, Payoff = 0. Overbidding is worse or equal.
    • If $B_{-i} < v_i$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff is $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. Bidding $v_i$ also wins and pays $B_{-i}$. Same payoff.
    $\implies$ Never better, sometimes worse than bidding $v_i$.
  2. Case 2: Consider bidding $b_i' < v_i$ (Underbidding).
    • If $B_{-i} \ge v_i$, you lose. Payoff = 0. (Same as bidding $v_i$).
    • If $b_i' \le B_{-i} < v_i$, you lose. Payoff = 0. Bidding $v_i$ means winning, Payoff = $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. Underbidding is worse.
    • If $B_{-i} < b_i'$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff is $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. Bidding $v_i$ also wins and pays $B_{-i}$. Same payoff.
    $\implies$ Never better, sometimes worse than bidding $v_i$.

Since bidding $b_i = v_i$ is weakly dominant, rational bidders will bid their true value.

Monetary Policy Debates: Gordon, the NKPC, and DIS Curve Insights

Posted on April 8, 2025 | Category: Macroeconomics, Monetary Policy

Modern monetary policy analysis often utilizes the New Keynesian framework, featuring a New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) and a Dynamic IS (DIS) relationship. Robert J. Gordon's work provides important context regarding inflation inertia and supply shocks.

The Standard New Keynesian Framework

NKPC: Relates inflation $\pi_t$ to expected inflation $E_t[\pi_{t+1}]$ and the output gap $y_t$.

$$ \pi_t = \beta E_t[\pi_{t+1}] + \kappa y_t + u_t $$

($\beta$: discount factor, $\kappa$: price stickiness parameter, $u_t$: cost-push shock)

DIS Curve: Links output gap $y_t$ to expected gap $E_t[y_{t+1}]$ and the real interest rate gap.

$$ y_t = E_t[y_{t+1}] - \sigma (i_t - E_t[\pi_{t+1}] - r^n_t) + v_t $$

($\sigma$: intertemporal substitution elasticity, $i_t$: nominal rate, $r^n_t$: natural rate, $v_t$: demand shock)

Gordon's Perspective & Policy Challenges

Gordon's "Triangle Model" emphasizes inertia (lagged inflation $\pi_{t-j}$), demand ($D_t$), and supply shocks ($Z_t$): $\pi_t = \sum \alpha_j \pi_{t-j} + \gamma D_t + \delta Z_t$. This highlights:

  • Strong Inflation Inertia: Observed persistence may exceed that implied by purely forward-looking NKPC models.
  • Role of Supply Shocks ($Z_t$): Direct impacts (e.g., oil prices) create policy trade-offs not fully captured by just $u_t$.
  • Measurement Uncertainty: Real-time estimation of potential output $Y_t^n$ and the natural rate $r^n_t$ (needed for gaps $y_t$ and $i_t - E_t[\pi_{t+1}] - r^n_t$) is difficult.

These factors suggest caution with simple policy rules and emphasize accounting for inertia, supply factors, and uncertainty.

Granger Causality in Time Series Analysis

Posted on April 9, 2025 | Category: Econometrics, Time Series

Clive Granger formalized a specific, testable notion of predictive causality for time series variables, known as Granger Causality.

Definition

A time series $X_t$ **Granger-causes** $Y_t$ if past values of $X_t$ help predict $Y_t$ beyond the information in past $Y_t$. This tests *predictability*, not necessarily structural causation.

Mathematical Formulation using VAR Models

Consider a bivariate VAR(p) model for stationary series $Y_t$ and $X_t$:

$$ Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \beta_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_{1t} $$ $$ X_t = \gamma_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \gamma_i Y_{t-i} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \delta_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_{2t} $$

where $\epsilon_{1t}, \epsilon_{2t}$ are white noise errors.

Testing for Granger Causality ($X \rightarrow Y$)

Hypotheses:

The null hypothesis $H_0$ is that $X$ does *not* Granger-cause $Y$ (all $\beta_i = 0$).

$$ H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \dots = \beta_p = 0 $$

The alternative $H_1$ is that at least one $\beta_i \neq 0$.

Test Procedure (F-test):

  1. Estimate the **Unrestricted Model** (first VAR equation above). Get $SSR_U$.
  2. Estimate the **Restricted Model** (excluding lagged $X$ terms): $Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \epsilon_{1t}^*$. Get $SSR_R$.
  3. Calculate the F-statistic: $$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_U) / p}{SSR_U / (T - k_U)} $$ where $p$ is the number of restrictions (lags of X), $T$ is sample size, $k_U = 2p+1$ is the number of parameters in the unrestricted model.
  4. Compare $F$ to $F_{critical}$ from $F(p, T-k_U)$ distribution. Reject $H_0$ if $F > F_{critical}$.

Rejecting $H_0$ suggests $X_t$ Granger-causes $Y_t$.

Important Considerations & Limitations

  • Requires stationary variables (or appropriate transformations/cointegration analysis).
  • Results depend on chosen lag length $p$ (use information criteria like AIC/BIC).
  • Prone to omitted variable bias.
  • Tests *predictive* ability, not necessarily structural causality.
