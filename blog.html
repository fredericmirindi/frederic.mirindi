Understanding Auction Mechanisms: First-Price vs. Second-Price Sealed Bids

Posted on April 8, 2025 | Category: Microeconomics, Game Theory

Auctions are common mechanisms for allocating goods. In sealed-bid auctions, participants submit bids simultaneously. Let's analyze bidder strategy in two key types, assuming independent private values where each bidder $i$ knows their own value $v_i$ but only the distribution of others' values.

1. First-Price Sealed-Bid Auction

The highest bidder wins and pays their own bid amount, $b_i$.

Strategic Considerations

Bidding your true value ($b_i = v_i$) is generally not optimal. If you win, your payoff is $v_i - b_i = v_i - v_i = 0$. Bidding lower ($b_i < v_i$) creates a potential positive payoff but reduces the probability of winning.

Equilibrium Bidding (Example)

Assume there are $n$ bidders whose values $v_i$ are independently drawn from a uniform distribution on $[0, 1]$. In a symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium, a bidder with value $v_i$ submits the optimal bid $b_i^*(v_i)$:

$$ b_i^*(v_i) = \frac{n-1}{n} v_i $$

This strategy involves "shading" the bid below the true value. The derivation involves maximizing the expected payoff $E[\text{Payoff}] = (v_i - b_i) \times P(\text{Win} | b_i)$, considering that others use the same strategy form.

2. Second-Price Sealed-Bid Auction (Vickrey Auction)

The highest bidder wins but pays the amount of the *second-highest* bid.

Dominant Strategy: Truthful Bidding

Bidding your true value ($b_i = v_i$) is a weakly dominant strategy. This means $b_i = v_i$ yields an expected payoff at least as high as any other bid $b_i'$, regardless of other players' bids.

Proof Outline (Why $b_i = v_i$ is optimal)

Let $v_i$ be your value and $B_{-i} = \max_{j \neq i} \{b_j\}$ be the highest bid among others.

  1. Case 1: Consider bidding $b_i' > v_i$ (Overbidding).
    • If $B_{-i} \ge b_i'$, you lose. Payoff = 0. (Same as bidding $v_i$).
    • If $v_i \le B_{-i} < b_i'$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff = $v_i - B_{-i} \le 0$. Bidding $v_i$ would mean losing, Payoff = 0. So, overbidding is worse or equal.
    • If $B_{-i} < v_i$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff = $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. Bidding $v_i$ also wins and pays $B_{-i}$. Same payoff.
    $\implies$ Never better, sometimes worse than bidding $v_i$.
  2. Case 2: Consider bidding $b_i' < v_i$ (Underbidding).
    • If $B_{-i} \ge v_i$, you lose. Payoff = 0. (Same as bidding $v_i$).
    • If $b_i' \le B_{-i} < v_i$, you lose. Payoff = 0. Bidding $v_i$ would mean winning and paying $B_{-i}$, Payoff = $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. So, underbidding is worse.
    • If $B_{-i} < b_i'$, you win and pay $B_{-i}$. Payoff = $v_i - B_{-i} > 0$. Bidding $v_i$ also wins and pays $B_{-i}$. Same payoff.
    $\implies$ Never better, sometimes worse than bidding $v_i$.

Since bidding $b_i = v_i$ yields a payoff greater than or equal to any other strategy $b_i'$ in all cases, it is a weakly dominant strategy.

Monetary Policy Debates: Gordon, the NKPC, and DIS Curve Insights

Posted on April 8, 2025 | Category: Macroeconomics, Monetary Policy

Modern monetary policy analysis often utilizes the New Keynesian framework, featuring a New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) and a Dynamic IS (DIS) relationship. Robert J. Gordon's work provides important context regarding inflation inertia and supply shocks.

The Standard New Keynesian Framework

New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC): Links current inflation ($\pi_t$) to expected future inflation ($E_t[\pi_{t+1}]$) and the output gap ($y_t$).

$$ \pi_t = \beta E_t[\pi_{t+1}] + \kappa y_t + u_t $$

where $\beta$ is the discount factor, $\kappa$ captures price stickiness, and $u_t$ represents cost-push shocks.

Dynamic IS Curve (DIS): Links the output gap ($y_t$) to its expected future value ($E_t[y_{t+1}]$) and the real interest rate gap.

$$ y_t = E_t[y_{t+1}] - \sigma (i_t - E_t[\pi_{t+1}] - r^n_t) + v_t $$

where $\sigma$ is intertemporal substitution elasticity, $i_t$ is the nominal policy rate, $r^n_t$ is the natural rate of interest, and $v_t$ represents demand shocks.

Gordon's Perspective & Policy Challenges

Gordon's "Triangle Model" highlights challenges for this framework:

  • Inflation Inertia: Gordon emphasized the role of past inflation ($\pi_{t-j}$) alongside demand ($D_t$) and supply shocks ($Z_t$): $\pi_t = \sum \alpha_j \pi_{t-j} + \gamma D_t + \delta Z_t$. This observed persistence can be harder to generate in purely forward-looking NKPC models.
  • Supply Shocks ($Z_t$): Significant impacts from supply factors (e.g., oil prices) complicate policy, as stabilizing inflation might worsen output gaps.
  • Measurement Uncertainty: Real-time estimation of potential output ($Y_t^n$) and the natural rate of interest ($r^n_t$) needed for $y_t$ and the interest rate gap is highly uncertain.

These factors suggest caution regarding simple policy rules derived solely from the NKPC/DIS model, emphasizing the need to account for inertia, supply factors, and measurement difficulties in practical policymaking.

Granger Causality in Time Series Analysis

Posted on April 9, 2025 | Category: Econometrics, Time Series

In time series econometrics, establishing causal relationships is challenging. Clive Granger formalized a specific, testable notion of predictive causality known as Granger Causality.

Definition

A time series variable $X_t$ is said to **Granger-cause** another variable $Y_t$ if past values of $X_t$ contain information that helps predict $Y_t$ beyond the information already contained in the past values of $Y_t$ itself.

Crucially, this is about *predictability*, not necessarily true economic causality in a structural sense. Correlation does not imply causation, and Granger causality primarily tests for predictive power.

Mathematical Formulation using VAR Models

Granger causality is typically tested within the framework of a Vector Autoregression (VAR) model. Consider a simple bivariate VAR(p) model with two stationary time series variables, $Y_t$ and $X_t$, and $p$ lags:

$$ Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \beta_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_{1t} $$ $$ X_t = \gamma_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \gamma_i Y_{t-i} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \delta_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_{2t} $$

where $\epsilon_{1t}$ and $\epsilon_{2t}$ are white noise error terms, potentially contemporaneously correlated.

Testing for Granger Causality ($X \rightarrow Y$)

To test if $X$ Granger-causes $Y$, we examine the statistical significance of the lagged coefficients of $X$ in the equation for $Y$.

Hypotheses:

The null hypothesis ($H_0$) is that $X$ does *not* Granger-cause $Y$. Mathematically, this means all coefficients on lagged $X$ terms in the $Y$ equation are zero:

$$ H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \dots = \beta_p = 0 $$

The alternative hypothesis ($H_1$) is that at least one $\beta_i \neq 0$.

Test Procedure (F-test):

  1. Estimate the **Unrestricted Model**: This is the full VAR equation for $Y_t$ as written above, including lags of both $Y$ and $X$. Obtain the Sum of Squared Residuals, $SSR_U$.
  2. Estimate the **Restricted Model**: This is the equation for $Y_t$ imposing the null hypothesis, meaning lags of $X$ are excluded: $$ Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \epsilon_{1t}^* $$ Obtain the Sum of Squared Residuals from this regression, $SSR_R$.
  3. Calculate the F-statistic: $$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_U) / p}{SSR_U / (T - 2p - 1)} $$ where $p$ is the number of lags (number of restrictions), $T$ is the number of observations in the sample, and $(T - 2p - 1)$ is the degrees of freedom for the unrestricted model.
  4. Compare to Critical Value: Compare the calculated F-statistic to the critical value from the F-distribution with $p$ and $(T - 2p - 1)$ degrees of freedom at a chosen significance level (e.g., 5%). If $F > F_{critical}$, reject $H_0$.

Rejecting $H_0$ implies that past values of $X_t$ significantly improve the prediction of $Y_t$, and we conclude that $X_t$ Granger-causes $Y_t$. A similar test can be performed for whether $Y$ Granger-causes $X$ using the second equation ($\gamma_i$ coefficients).

Important Considerations & Limitations

  • Stationarity: Variables in the VAR should generally be stationary. Non-stationary data requires careful handling (e.g., cointegration analysis, differencing).
  • Lag Length ($p$): The choice of lag length $p$ is crucial and can affect results. Information criteria (AIC, BIC, HQIC) are often used to select $p$.
  • Omitted Variables: If a third variable $Z_t$ influences both $X_t$ and $Y_t$, a significant Granger causality test result between $X$ and $Y$ might be spurious, reflecting the influence of the omitted $Z_t$.
  • Instantaneous Causality: Granger causality tests only past predictive power. It doesn't capture contemporaneous relationships (often examined via the correlation of residuals $\epsilon_{1t}$ and $\epsilon_{2t}$).
  • "Causality" Definition: It's vital to remember this is *predictive* causality within the context of the model, not necessarily a deep structural or philosophical causal link.

Despite its limitations, the Granger causality test is a valuable tool in the time series econometrician's toolkit for exploring dynamic relationships between variables.
