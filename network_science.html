Can Network Science Predict the New Pope? Insights from the 2025 Conclave

Posted on May 9, 2025 | Category: Religion, Network Science

1. The Power of Networks in the Papal Election

Source: The Washington Post

The election of a new pope is one of the world’s most secretive and consequential decisions. While spiritual discernment is at its heart, recent research from Bocconi University shows that social dynamics-status, alliances, and information flow among cardinals-play a crucial role in shaping the outcome. This year’s conclave, which brought together 133 cardinal electors from 70 countries, offered a real-world test for the predictive power of network science in understanding papal succession[5][6].

2. How Network Science Illuminates the Conclave

The Bocconi study mapped the Vatican’s relational ecosystem using data on official roles, spiritual lineages, and informal connections. It identified three main factors that determine a cardinal’s prominence:

  • Status: Institutional authority and visibility within the Church.
  • Information Control: Ability to bridge and connect different groups.
  • Coalition-Building: Embeddedness within cohesive groups and capacity to unite factions.

This approach doesn’t claim to predict the outcome with certainty, but it offers a new lens to understand why certain cardinals become frontrunners[5][6].

Source: Bocconi University Network Conclave Study

4. The 2025 Conclave: Network Science Meets Reality

This year’s conclave confirmed the value of network analysis. Despite media speculation favoring Italian or Asian candidates, the election of a moderate, well-connected cardinal-who bridged several ideological groups-mirrored the predictions of the Bocconi model. The new pope’s rise was less about public profile and more about his centrality in the Church’s intricate web of relationships.

“We’re not trying to forecast the future... This model is meant to offer perspective, not prophecy.”
- Professor Giuseppe Soda, Bocconi University[6]

As the Church continues to globalize and diversify, understanding the conclave as a networked system will only become more relevant for scholars, journalists, and the faithful alike.
